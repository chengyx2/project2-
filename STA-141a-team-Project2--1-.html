Lets look at the daily number of new cases in the US, China and globally since Jan 3, 2020.

covid %>% group_by(Date_reported) %>% filter(Country=="China") %>%
  summarise(new_cases = sum(New_cases)) %>%
  ggplot(aes(y=new_cases, x=Date_reported)) +
  geom_line() + 
  ggtitle("Number of new cases (China)")

We can see that the number of new cases reaches a maximum around Jan 2021 and then decreases. Recently, we are interested in what reason cause the number of new cases decreases. After we looking for information online, its possibly because that there are several new type of covid-19 vaccines published, and people are getting vaccinated. In the United States, the number of new cases has a similar trend as the global data as most of countries globally. In China, the number of new cases exploded in February 2020, but after that it maintained at a low level, it controlled the covid-19 very well as a few other countries did. This suggests that the pandemic developed differently in different countries.

However, there has a significant data outlier for china that there has one day increased over 15000 new cases, which takes up almost half of total new numbers. After searching we find that this is because chinese government report the cases which hasnt been diagnosed earily.

An interesting fact we find is that the region for top-10 low mortality rate countries distributed equally in seven regions, but the region for top-10 high mortality rate countries are distributed more concentrated on East Mediterranean countreis and Latin America. 5 Inferential Analysis

We are going to answer the question: Does case-mortality rate differ among different regions? WHO divided countries into 7 regions: Regional Office for Africa (AFRO), Regional Office for the Americas (AMRO), Regional Office for the Eastern Mediterranean (EMRO), Regional Office for Europe (EURO), Regional Office for South East Asia (SEARO), Regional Office for the Western Pacific (WPRO) and other. We are going to use an ANOVA model to compare the case-mortality rate among these regions. The model is:

\[y = \beta_0 + \beta_1AFRO+\beta_2AMRO+\beta_3EMRO+\beta_4EURO+\beta_5SEARO+\beta_6WPRO+\epsilon\]

\(y\) is the case-mortality rate, and there are six dummy variables for the six regions. Other region is chosen as the baseline group.

covid_motality_rate$WHO_region <- relevel(factor(covid_motality_rate$WHO_region), ref="Other")
m <- aov(rate~WHO_region, covid_motality_rate)

This means that the Eastern Mediterranean region has a significantly higher case-mortality rate than Europe, South East Asia and Western Pacific regions.

7 Conclusion

In summary, we conducted research in this report around COVID-19, a highly infectious virus. Outbreaks of COVID-19 have led to the deaths of many infected people. We are eager to understand how the pandemic is developing on a global scale, to find links between the death toll and different countries and regions, and to adopt a positive response. Our research questions included Do the case fatality rates differ from region to region? When did the number of new cases peak? Which country has the highest number of deaths? Its pretty much settled. First, from descriptive analysis, we have the trend of new cases and deaths since January 3, 2020. We found that the number of new cases and deaths peaked around January 2021 and then declined. This could be because more people are being vaccinated. Subsequently, in the Inferential Analysis section, we used the ANOVA model and compared the case fatality rates in seven regions. After removing 2 outliers, we concluded that there were significant differences in case fatality rates between the 7 regions. To further investigate exactly which regions are most affected, we use Tukeys confidence interval. We found that the case fatality rate was significantly lower in the Western Pacific than in the Eastern Mediterranean. This means that the Eastern Mediterranean region will be more affected by the pandemic than the Western Pacific region and may need more help if WHO decides to allocate resources to mitigate the pandemic. The results of the study are very important, and the practicality and constructiveness of the study are worth our consideration. Based on the analysis results and the existing authoritative literature, we can basically infer the more general conclusion that the number of deaths is related to different countries and regions, and the case fatality rate is different in different regions. We can learn more about this finding in the future and apply it to the global fight against the pandemic. For example, we are seeing a gradual decline in the number of new cases and deaths. It is reasonable to speculate that this may be because more people are being vaccinated. If we can further verify that the vaccine has a positive effect on COVID-19 mortality, we can recommend that the vaccine be used in all regions of the world to improve the situation. In addition, we know that the western Pacific region has a significantly lower case fatality rate than the eastern Mediterranean region and is more affected by the pandemic than the western Pacific region. The reason behind it is worth further study. Different regions have different geographical environments and national conditions, which may include economic conditions, medical facilities, government policies, etc. These are constructive questions.
